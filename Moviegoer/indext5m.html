TOP 50 FAVOURITE MOTION PICTURES

50. THE BREAKFAST CLUB

Plot: Five high school students from different walks of life endure a Saturday detention under a power-hungry principal. The disparate group includes rebel John, princess Claire, outcast Allison, brainy Brian and Andrew, the jock. Each has a chance to tell his or her story, making the others see them a little differently -- and when the day ends, they question whether school will ever be the same.

Cast: John Hughes, Emilio Estevez, Paul Gleason, Anthony Michael Hall, Judd Nelson

Genre: Drama/Comedy-Drama

Year: 1985

Director: John Hughes

Producer: Ned Tanen, John Hughes

Writer: John Hughes

Duration: 97 minutes

Language: English

Country: United States

Production Company: Channel Productions

Budget: 1 Million USD

Box Office: 51.5 Million USD

Awards Won: 2

Critic Score:
- Rotten Tomatoes: 88%
- IMDb: 7.9/10

User Score: 94%

Critic Review: Teenage alienation? Check. Angst? Check. Ennui? (Even though we didn't even really know what that was in 1985?) Check. (Adam R. Holz, Plugged In)

Audience Review: I would most certainly recommend it. Although many parts of it are predictable and extreme caricatures of social and economic strata, I loved the way that all the characters, including the teacher were shaken, and stirred, and realised that they had more in common than they had initially concluded.

45. WAR HORSE

Plot: Albert and his beloved horse, Joey, live on a farm in the British countryside. At the outbreak of World War, I, Albert and Joey are forcibly parted when Albert's father sells the horse to the British cavalry. Against the backdrop of the Great War, Joey begins an odyssey full of danger, joy and sorrow, and he transforms everyone he meets along the way. Meanwhile Albert, unable to forget his equine friend, searches the battlefields of France to find Joey and bring him home.

Cast: Emily Watson, David Thewlis, Peter Mullan, Niels Arestrup, Jeremy Irvine

Genre: Drama/War

Year: 2011

Budget: 66 million USD

Critic Review: War Horse is bold, exquisite family filmmaking in the grandest Hollywood tradition. Be warned: whether you're a hippophilic or not, it's a four-hankie moviegoing experience. (Ian Freer, Empire)

Like the Kubrick film, this is a gorgeous piece of work, with each shot looking like something straight out of a painting. This is easily some of the best cinematography from any of Spielberg's films.

But increased pressure from the police, wars with Colombian drug cartels and his own drug-fuelled paranoia serve to fuel the flames of his eventual downfall.

There, he is forced to deal with a past that separated him from his wife, Randi, and the community where he was born and raised.
